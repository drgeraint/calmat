Kepler's Laws

Johannes Kepler

  • used Tycho Brahe’s (1546-1601) accurate measurements of positions of planets as they move across the background of stars. He had access to 20 years of observation data, especially of Mars.
  • tried 70 different combinations of circles and circles within circles to model the observed motions - finally abandoned circles.
  • tried ovals, where the speed of the planet changed as it went around the oval.
  • after 7 years, discovered an ellipse fitted the observations.
  • Kepler's laws are based purely on the observations.

Kepler’s First Law:

Each planet’s path around the Sun is an ellipse, with the Sun at one focus of the ellipse.

Kepler’s Second Law:

A planet moves along its elliptical path with a speed that changes in such a way that a line from the planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time.
So a planet moves faster when it’s close to the Sun, slower when farther away.

Kepler’s Third Law:
The ratio of the cube of a planet’s average distance from the Sun to the square of its orbital period is the same for each planet
This ratio is constant for every body that revolves around the same central body
eg. constant for all satellites about Jupiter, and for all planets about the Sun.

a = average distance from planet to Sun
P = period of planet's orbit (observable)
